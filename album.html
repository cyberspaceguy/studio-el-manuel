Gorillaz Reach a New High with 'The Mountain'

By Emmanuel Mutaka | Art Critic

If you’ve been following Gorillaz lately, you know they’ve spent a lot of time in the neon-lit world of synth-pop. But with their ninth studio album, The Mountain, the band has stepped out of the club and into the sunlight. For those of us who live for the pure musicality of a project, this album is a masterpiece of instrumental layering and melodic songwriting.

The record trades the cold pulse of drum machines for an organic landscape. The inclusion of traditional Indian instruments like the sitar and sarod isn't just window dressing—they are woven into the DNA of the tracks, creating a psychedelic tapestry that feels both ancient and futuristic. You can practically hear the "fingers on the strings," giving the project a tactile, soulful quality.

The track "QuickStop" is a definitive highlight, captivating with its storytelling that paints vivid pictures. It feels like being immersed in a television show, playing directly through your ears. The narrative depth here proves that Damon Albarn isn't just a curator, but a master of the "episodic" song structure.

Additionally, the song "Safety" offers a beautiful, melodic reflection on friendships and life back home. It grounds the album’s high-concept sounds with a very modern, relatable mood. Overall, The Mountain provides a real hip-hop experience filtered through a melodic lens—unapologetically catchy, yet deeply sophisticated.

Critique Notes

Vivid Imagery

Lyrical detail in tracks like "QuickStop" makes you a spectator in the narrative rather than just a listener.

Melodic Dominance

Unlike previous synth-heavy releases, this album prioritizes soaring harmonies and acoustic "earworm" hooks.

Atmospheric Instrumentation

The use of harmonic, organic instrumentals gives the project a soulful foundation that ties the global sounds together.

Narrative Variety

Every track feels like a different "episode," balancing high-energy storytelling with reflective, melodic pauses.
